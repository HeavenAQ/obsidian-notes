## Reading Summary

**Abstract**

This PLOS ONE study from the Leipzig Haptic Research Laboratory is the first to measure the temporal microstructure of spontaneous facial self-touch (sFST) to the millisecond, using accelerometers and EMG rather than video coding. Sixty participants performed a delayed haptic memory task under aversive acoustic distraction, and the authors show that not only the frequency but also the contact duration and the point of touch of sFST shift systematically with cognitive and emotional load.

**Research Question**

Do the frequency, movement times, contact durations, hand preference, and facial point of touch of spontaneous (non-instrumental) facial self-touches vary with cognitive load (memory retention) and emotional load (aversive sounds)?

**Methodology**

60 participants manually explored complex sunken-relief stimuli, retained them over a 14-minute retention interval (RI) filled with 40 aversive sounds alternating with 40 silences (7–13 s each, jittered to prevent habituation), then reproduced them on paper — twice. sFST were recorded with wrist accelerometers and forearm EMG, decomposed into movement toward the face (T1), skin contact (T2), and movement away (T3); instrumental touches (scratching, yawning, etc.) and touches to hair/head/neck/ears were excluded, as were events over 10 s. Analyses compared RI vs. other phases, sound (IN) vs. silence (OUT) periods, hands, and face regions.

**Findings**

Self-touch is load-sensitive on every measured axis except hand choice. sFST clustered in the high-load retention interval and, within it, were more frequent during aversive sounds; but contact durations were longest in the silences between sounds — suggesting brief "regulatory" touches under acute distraction versus sustained touches during focused rehearsal. Touches concentrated on the midline of the face during sounds and dispersed across regions during silences; ipsilateral touches dominated. Hand use was symmetric, contradicting the lateralized-emotion hypothesis (H2). The authors interpret sFST as tied to cognitive focusing and attentional regulation rather than purely negative affect.

**Results**

54/60 participants produced sFST (M = 5.61 per person, SD = 4.81); mean contact duration T2 = 1.76 s (SD = 1.53), with T1 ≈ 0.91 s and T3 ≈ 0.98 s. Frequency: 194 sFST during RI vs. 109 in all other phases combined (binomial p < .001); within RI, 131 during sounds vs. 62 during silences (p < .001; within-subjects t(24) = 3.78, p < .001, d = 0.76). Durations: contact longer OUT than IN (t(18) = −2.80, p < .05, d = 0.64) and OUT longer than other phases (t(18) = 3.36, p < .005, d = 0.77). Hand use: right 164 vs. left 130 (p = .054, n.s.); midline of face touched most during sounds (middle vs. left p < .01).

**Conclusion**

The authors conclude that contact duration and point of touch — not just frequency — are modulated by cognitive and emotional demands, and that sFST likely serves cognitive/attentional regulation. For the thesis this is prime RQ1 evidence: it justifies treating fine-grained self-adaptor dynamics (duration, location, timing relative to load events) as signals of processing difficulty, and it directly motivates the Layer-2 pipeline requirement that detection be contact-aware and temporally precise rather than a coarse "touching/not-touching" classifier — the behavioral bridge to cognitive-load estimation in the DENSO framing.
