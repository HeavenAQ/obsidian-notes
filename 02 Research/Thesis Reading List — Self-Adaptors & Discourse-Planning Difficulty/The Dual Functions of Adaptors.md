## Reading Summary

**Abstract**

Lopez-Ozieblo (Languages, 2025 — same special issue as Sekine & Hotta) analyzes self-adaptors in 40 Cantonese L2-English speakers across monologues and dialogues, face-to-face and online. The paper argues that adaptors are not mere emotional leakage: "other adaptors" serve cognitive self-regulatory functions while a newly foregrounded micro-movement category, "flutters," serves interactive/engagement functions. This directly challenges the Ekman-Friesen view of adaptors as non-communicative.

**Research Question**

Do adaptors and flutters vary with (1) context (face-to-face vs. online), (2) task (monologue vs. dialogue), (3) task familiarity (session 1 vs. 2), and (4) speaking vs. listening role — and what does that patterning imply about their function (emotional, cognitive, or interactive)?

**Methodology**

Forty Cantonese speakers of English as L2 performed cartoon-retelling monologues and dialogues in face-to-face and online conditions. Adaptor frequency was measured per 100 words; flutters (sustained micro hand/finger movements) as duration over speaking time. Non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney U between contexts, Wilcoxon signed-rank within pairs) with Holm-Bonferroni corrections and rank-biserial effect sizes; participant self-report interviews corroborated interpretations.

**Findings**

Other adaptors and flutters dissociate cleanly. Other adaptors were stable across speaking roles and contexts but were more frequent in monologues — tasks demanding solo reconstitution of content without interlocutor feedback — supporting a cognitive-load/self-regulation function and not an anxiety function (production trends fell, not rose, with familiarity, and anxiety measures showed null effects). Flutters instead increased markedly in online dialogues, suggesting they index interactional strain/engagement — compensatory behavior where feedback signals and turn-taking rhythms are degraded. The paper proposes a dual-function model: other adaptors = cognitive regulators; flutters = interactive/attentional signals; and argues the classic duration-based ("three-second rule") classification of adaptors is inadequate.

**Results**

Speakers produced significantly fewer other adaptors per 100 words in dialogues (M = 1.67) than monologues (M = 2.84; W = 198, adjusted p = .037, r = .43). Flutter duration while speaking was significantly longer in online dialogues (M = 0.23) than face-to-face (M = 0.12; U = 304, adjusted p = .01, r = .44). Listening adaptors were higher face-to-face than online (adjusted p = .03). Online monologues showed lower flutter rates than online dialogues (adjusted p = .027, r = .65). Familiarity effects (adaptors down, flutters up across sessions) were directional but non-significant.

**Conclusion**

The author concludes that adaptors must be included in gesture analysis under a functional (not duration-based) taxonomy: other adaptors anchor the speaker cognitively during demanding verbal tasks, flutters negotiate interaction. For the thesis this is direct Layer-1/RQ1 ammunition: it is current, peer-reviewed evidence that self-adaptors track discourse-planning/cognitive demand rather than anxiety, and its monologue-vs-dialogue manipulation is a ready-made behavioral contrast to operationalize. It also cautions that the detection pipeline (RQ2) should distinguish sustained micro-fidgeting (flutters) from discrete self-touch events, since they carry different signals.
