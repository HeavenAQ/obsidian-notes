**Background & research question.**  Do self-adaptors facilitate lexical access during word search? 

**Methodology.**  Experimental manipulation comparing the presence vs suppression of self-touch during retrieval/naming tasks. 

**Conclusion.**  Self-adaptors aid lexical retrieval—the closest direct linguistic hypothesis for this thesis —pointing to word searches, prolonged pauses, and self-repairs as the first downstream targets.

## Reading Summary

**Abstract**

This experimental study asks whether self-adaptor behaviors — non-communicative bodily actions such as touching one's face or clasping one's hands — facilitate lexical retrieval. Sixty Japanese-speaking adults performed a definition-to-word retrieval task designed to elicit tip-of-the-tongue (TOT) states under one of three conditions: a self-adaptor condition (holding their cheeks), a suppression condition (hand movement inhibited by holding a cardboard tube), and a free-movement control. The self-adaptor condition produced significantly more correct responses than the suppression condition, but self-adaptors did not improve TOT resolution or later recall.

**Research Question**

Do self-adaptor behaviours causally support lexical access during word retrieval difficulty (Hypothesis 1), and do they specifically aid the resolution of tip-of-the-tongue states (Hypothesis 2)? A secondary question is how spontaneous self-adaptors and gestures distribute across response outcomes when movement is unconstrained.

**Methodology**

Between-subjects experiment, N = 60 (20 per condition). Participants heard spoken definitions of 30 low-frequency Japanese items (proverbs, four-character idioms) and had up to one minute to produce each target word, then completed a surprise recall task. Conditions: self-adaptor (instructed cheek-holding), suppression (both hands holding a 50.5 cm cardboard tube), control (free movement). Responses were coded as successful retrieval, retrieval failure, TOT success, or TOT failure; in the control condition, video was annotated in ELAN, with self-adaptors classified into 16 anatomical regions (Sugawara 1987) and gestures into McNeill's four types. Analyses were one-way ANOVAs with Bonferroni post hocs, plus correlational analysis of movement frequency and TOT resolution.

**Findings**

Instructed self-adaptor behaviour facilitated overall lexical retrieval relative to suppression, but had no effect on the likelihood of entering a TOT state, on resolving a TOT once entered, or on delayed recall. In free-moving speakers, self-adaptors were far more frequent than gestures overall and increased when lexical access became difficult, while gestures spiked specifically during TOT states. The authors interpret self-adaptors as attention-regulation devices that stabilize cognition under retrieval load rather than as direct memory aids.

**Results**

Mean correct responses (of 30): self-adaptor 13.05 (SD 4.96) vs. suppression 8.45 (SD 3.90) vs. control 10.70 (SD 6.30); main effect F(2,57) = 3.99, p = .024, η²p = .12; self-adaptor vs. suppression t(57) = 2.83, p = .019, d = 0.88 (also significant when TOT successes were excluded). No effects on TOT frequency (F = 1.16, p = .32), TOT success rate (F = 1.41, p = .25, so H2 unsupported), or recall (F = 0.30, p = .74). In the control condition, self-adaptors averaged 2.20 per trial for successful retrievals vs. 3.90 for TOT successes and 3.55 for TOT failures; gestures rose from ~0.005 per trial (successful retrieval) to 0.50 (TOT success). TOT successes correlated with self-adaptor frequency (r = .95, p < .001) and gesture frequency (r = .51, p < .05).

**Conclusion**

Self-adaptors contribute functionally to immediate lexical retrieval — likely by enhancing cognitive focus or alleviating retrieval interference — but not to TOT resolution or memory consolidation, so the functions of different spontaneous body movements must be distinguished. For the thesis, this is the most direct causal evidence linking self-adaptors to word-search difficulty, and it motivates pauses, word-searches, and self-repairs as the discourse-side signals to align with self-adaptor events.
