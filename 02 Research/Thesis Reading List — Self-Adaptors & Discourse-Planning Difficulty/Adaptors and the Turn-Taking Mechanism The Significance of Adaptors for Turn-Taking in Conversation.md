## Reading Summary

**Abstract**
The paper investigates whether adaptors — nonverbal behaviors involving self-touch or manipulation of objects, traditionally treated as private, self-regulatory behavior unrelated to communication — play a functional role in the mechanics of conversational turn-taking. Analyzing semi-scripted dyadic conversations, the authors annotate discrete adaptor events and turn borders and test whether adaptors cluster around floor transfers. They find that adaptors occur significantly more often near turn boundaries than would be expected by chance, challenging the standard view that adaptors are communicatively inert.

**Research Question**
Do adaptors, historically framed as purely intrapersonal, self-regulatory gestures, also serve an interactional function in managing the timing and organization of conversational turns — specifically, are they systematically distributed relative to turn-transition points rather than occurring at random throughout speech?

**Methodology**
The authors recorded 10 semi-scripted dyadic conversations (12 participants total) and manually annotated the video/audio for two categories of events: discrete adaptors (self-touches, object manipulation) and turn borders (floor transfers between speakers). They then analyzed the temporal distribution of adaptor onsets relative to turn-boundary events, testing whether adaptors are overrepresented near turn transitions compared to elsewhere in the conversation.

**Findings**
Adaptors were not uniformly distributed across the conversation but instead clustered significantly around floor transfers, suggesting they are implicated in turn-taking coordination — potentially functioning as cues related to floor-holding, turn-yielding, or the interactional work of managing the exchange of speaking rights. This runs counter to the long-standing ethological/clinical tradition (dating to Ekman & Friesen) that treats adaptors as private regulatory behavior with no signal value to the interlocutor.

**Results**
This is a hypothesis-driven corpus/distributional-analysis paper rather than a classifier-benchmark paper: its core reported result is a statistically significant excess frequency of discrete adaptors in the vicinity of turn borders relative to non-boundary regions of the conversation. The vault materials available for this paper do not carry the underlying test statistics (e.g., exact p-values or effect sizes) beyond confirming significance, so this summary is based on the abstract and secondary description rather than the full paper text.

**Conclusion**
The authors conclude that adaptors participate in social/interactional coordination rather than being purely self-regulatory, situating self-touch within the "interaction engine" of conversation and offering a functional bridge between individual cognitive/interactional load and turn management. For the thesis, this is core Layer 1 evidence: it directly supports the RQ1 hypothesis that self-adaptors are not epiphenomenal but are systematically tied to moments of interactional/cognitive difficulty (turn transitions being a locus of discourse-planning demand), and it motivates treating turn-position as a key covariate in the contact-aware detection pipeline (Layer 2).
