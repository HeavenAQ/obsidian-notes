### 1. Emblems

- **Emblems are nonverbal acts with a clear verbal meaning.** 
    - They can usually be translated directly into a word, short phrase, or dictionary-like definition.
- **Their meaning is shared within a group or culture.** 
    - People in that community recognize what the gesture means, including smaller groups that use private or secret signs.
- **They can support, replace, or contradict speech.** 
    - For example, an emblem may repeat what someone says, stand in for words when speech is unavailable, or communicate the opposite of the spoken message.
- **They are usually deliberate and consciously used to communicate.** 
    - People generally know they made the emblem, can repeat it, and are responsible for its message—though accidental “emblematic slips” can happen.
- **They are especially useful when verbal communication is difficult or impossible**
    - such as in loud places, over distance, during charades, or for people with speech or hearing impairments.
- **They are among the easiest nonverbal behaviors for others to understand.** 
    - People may even directly comment on or respond to an emblem’s meaning.
- **Emblems are learned and often culture-specific.** 
    - Different cultures can have different emblem systems, although some meanings may be understandable across cultures.
- **They may be arbitrary or iconic.** 
    - An arbitrary emblem does not visually resemble its meaning, while an iconic emblem looks like or imitates what it represents—for example, tracing a body shape or shaking a fist.

### 2. Illustrator

- **Illustrators are movements that are directly connected to speech.** 
    - They help show, emphasize, or clarify what a person is saying at the same moment.
- **Unlike emblems, illustrators depend strongly on accompanying speech.** 
    - Their meaning is often tied to the words, tone, rhythm, loudness, or content of what is being said.
- **Illustrators can repeat, replace, contradict, or add to verbal information.** 
    - For example, a person might point while saying “over there,” or gesture broadly to make an explanation more vivid.
- **There are six main types of illustrators:**
    - **Batons:** rhythmic movements that emphasize a word or phrase, like beating time with a hand.
    - **Ideographs:** movements that trace the direction or progression of an idea, such as moving a hand forward while describing a process.
    - **Deictic movements:** pointing to a present object, person, or location.
    - **Spatial movements:** showing a spatial relationship, size, position, or distance.
    - **Kinetographs:** depicting a bodily, mechanical, or natural action through movement.
    - **Pictographs:** drawing a visual picture of the thing being discussed.
> **Batons and ideographs usually have little meaning by themselves.** 
Without hearing the speech, an observer may not know what they mean. In contrast, pointing, action-imitation, spatial gestures, and picture-like gestures can often convey part of the message even without sound.
- **Illustrators are usually intentional, but people may be less aware of using them than they are of emblems.** 
    - They often occur naturally while someone is speaking, especially when the person is engaged, excited, or explaining something.
- **They are socially learned.** 
    - Children often acquire them by imitating people they identify with, and public-speaking instruction can also teach particular illustrative gestures.
- **Illustrator styles can vary across cultural and ethnic groups.** 
    - For example, the text describes differences in the types of gestures used by Italian and Jewish immigrant groups, although those differences became less visible among more assimilated descendants.
- **Most illustrators are iconic or intrinsic.** 
    - They either resemble what they represent, such as miming an action, or directly show it, such as pointing at an object.
- **Illustrators may help make speech more understandable and engaging.** 
    - They draw visual attention and give listeners additional information about the speaker’s meaning, emphasis, direction of thought, or emotional involvement.

### 3. Affect Displays

- **Affect displays are nonverbal expressions of emotion, primarily shown through the face.**
    - Facial muscle movements are treated as the main site of emotional display, while many body movements are viewed more as responses to emotion than the emotion display itself.
- **The text proposes several primary affects:** 
    - happiness, surprise, fear, sadness, anger, disgust, and interest. These emotions are thought to have distinctive facial-muscle patterns.
- **Some facial expressions may be universal across cultures.** 
    - The authors argue that people in different cultures can often recognize the same basic emotional expressions, although the universality claim was still presented as tentative and in need of further evidence.
- **Culture may shape what causes an emotion, rather than the basic facial pattern itself.**
    - For example, the same event may trigger sadness in one culture but another emotion in a different culture, so researchers should not assume that the same situation produces the same feeling everywhere.
- **People learn “display rules” that manage how emotions appear on the face.** 
    - These rules may lead someone to reduce an expression, exaggerate it, appear neutral, or mask one emotion with another—for example, looking happy while feeling afraid.
- **Display rules vary by culture and social context.** 
    - Expectations about showing emotion can depend on factors such as social role, status, age, sex, the people present, and the situation.
- **Faces often express blends of emotions rather than only one pure emotion.** 
    - A person may show two or more emotions simultaneously, or move rapidly from one emotion to another.
- **Affect displays may be intentional or unintentional.** 
    - People can deliberately control, hide, or fake facial expressions, but genuine emotion can also “leak” through involuntary facial movements.
- **Affect displays often reveal more personal information than emblems or illustrators.** 
    - A person’s emotional blends, sequences, triggers, and ways of controlling expression can reveal individual habits and social experience.
- **Facial expressions can relate to speech in several ways.** 
    - They may repeat, qualify, contradict, or be separate from what is said verbally; a culturally recognized expression, such as a smile, can also function as an emblem.
- **The authors developed a facial coding system, FAST, to identify facial cues associated with primary emotions.** 
    - Their cross-cultural studies reported substantial agreement in recognition of several emotions, supporting the possibility of shared facial-emotion patterns across cultures.

### 4. Regulators

- **Regulators are nonverbal behaviors that manage the flow of conversation.** 
    - They help coordinate the back-and-forth process of speaking and listening between people.
- **They signal how the conversation should proceed.** 
    - Regulators can tell someone to continue, repeat, explain more, speak faster, let someone else speak, pay attention, wait, or begin talking.
- **Common examples include head nods, eye contact, leaning slightly forward, small posture changes, and eyebrow raises.** 
    - A head nod, for example, can function like saying “mm-hmm” to encourage the speaker to continue.
- **Regulators are different from illustrators.** 
    - Illustrators are closely tied to the meaning, rhythm, or emphasis of particular words, while regulators guide the overall pacing and turn-taking of the conversation.
- **Most regulators do not have independent message content.** 
    - Instead of conveying a specific idea, they provide information needed to keep communication smooth and coordinated.
- **Many other behaviors can have a regulative effect, but they are only called regulators when regulation is their main function.** 
    - For example, an emotional expression may influence another person, but it is not necessarily a regulator unless it mainly manages the interaction.
- **People are usually only partly aware of using regulators.** 
    - Someone may nod or shift posture without consciously deciding to do so, but can usually recognize and repeat the behavior if asked.
- **Listeners are often very sensitive to the absence of regulators.** 
    - When a person stops nodding, making eye contact, or giving other conversational signals, the speaker may become uncomfortable and the conversation can break down.
- **Regulators are usually less deliberate than emblems and illustrators.** 
    - They are often automatic, highly learned habits rather than conscious attempts to control communication.
- **Regulators may differ across cultures, social groups, settings, and roles.** 
    - Differences in expected eye contact, nodding, posture, or conversational timing can lead people to see someone as rude or uninterested, even when they are simply following a different regulator system.
- **Scheflen describes three larger levels of regulators:**
    - **Points:** small head, eye, or neck movements that mark the end of a larger conversational unit or signal a conversational point.
    - **Positions:** broader patterns of posture and distance that express a person’s stance or point of view in the interaction.
    - **Presentations:** the overall organization of posture, orientation, and distance that shapes the intimacy, inclusiveness, and focus of the entire interaction.
- **Regulators are always interactive.** 
    - Their main role is to influence how the other person participates, even though they are not usually intentional messages in the same way as words or emblems.

### 5. Adaptor

- **Adaptors are habitual movements that originally developed to meet bodily, emotional, interpersonal, or practical needs.** 
    - They may have begun as complete actions in childhood, but later appear as small fragments of those original actions during adult interaction.
- **They are usually not intended as messages.** 
    - Adaptors tend to happen automatically, with little awareness, when something in the current situation triggers an old learned habit connected to a need, emotion, relationship, or setting.
- **There are three main types of adaptors:**
    - **Self-adaptors:** movements directed toward oneself.
    - **Alter-adaptors:** movements rooted in interaction with another person.
    - **Object-adaptors:** movements originally learned while using an object or performing a practical task.
- <u>**Self-adaptors involve managing or responding to one’s own body and needs.**</u><u> </u>
    - <u>They can relate to sensory input, eating and drinking, grooming, speech, bodily comfort, or self-soothing. Examples may include touching the face, licking or wiping the lips, rubbing the eyes, scratching the head, or holding part of the body.</u>
- <u>**In conversation, self-adaptors are often reduced versions of earlier behaviors.**</u><u> </u>
    - <u>Someone may touch their face or mouth not because they are literally grooming or eating, but because an emotional state or situation has triggered a related habit.</u>
- <u>**Hand-to-face movements are especially important self-adaptors.**</u><u> </u>
    - <u>The authors treat the face as psychologically connected to the self, so touching, covering, rubbing, scratching, or holding the face may provide clues about reassurance, avoidance, self-attack, support, thought, sadness, or other personal states.</u>
- <u>**Their meanings can be partly shared but are also highly personal.**</u><u> </u>
    - <u>A movement may have a broadly recognizable meaning, but the specific emotion, memory, relationship, or situation that triggers it can depend on the individual’s childhood experiences and life history.</u>
- **Alter-adaptors originate in early interpersonal actions.** 
    - They include fragments of movements related to giving, taking, protecting oneself, attacking, seeking affection, withdrawing, flirting, sexual invitation, or escaping an interaction.
- **Some restless body movements may be alter-adaptors rather than simple signs of anxiety.** 
    - For instance, leg, hand, or foot movements can reflect residues of aggression, sexual invitation, flight, or a wish to withdraw from the interaction.
- **Object-adaptors come from learned practical activities involving objects.** 
    - Actions originally connected to driving, smoking, using tools, or similar tasks may reappear partially during conversation when a related emotional or attitudinal state is activated.
- **Adaptors are usually iconic or intrinsic rather than arbitrary.** 
    - Their meaning comes from their connection to an action or activity they partly resemble or reproduce. Because they are learned through bodily and social experience, many adaptors may vary across cultures.
- **Adaptors may reveal information people are trying not to show.**
    - Because they are often outside conscious control, the authors view them as a possible source of “leakage” during deceptive or emotionally charged interactions.

## Reading Summary

**Abstract**

This is the foundational taxonomy paper that proposes a system for classifying nonverbal behavior based on three analytic dimensions — origin, usage, and coding. It introduces five major categories of nonverbal act (emblems, illustrators, affect displays, regulators, and adaptors) and is the paper that formally coins "adaptors," including the self-adaptor / alter-adaptor / object-adaptor distinction still used throughout the self-touch literature.

**Research Question**

How can any instance of nonverbal behavior be systematically classified so that its origin (how the behavior became part of a person's repertoire), usage (the circumstances, awareness, intentionality, and function of its use), and coding (the rules by which it carries information — arbitrary, iconic, or intrinsic) can be understood within one unified analytic scheme?

**Methodology**

The paper is conceptual/theoretical rather than a single controlled experiment. Ekman and Friesen synthesize roughly eight years of their own empirical, cross-cultural observational research (including studies of nonverbal leakage and deception, and cross-cultural comparisons of nonverbal behavior) into a taxonomy. Each category is defined and justified along the origin/usage/coding dimensions through inductive analysis of naturalistic nonverbal behavior drawn from these prior studies rather than through a new standalone experiment.

**Findings**

Five categories are proposed: emblems (culture-specific gestures with a direct verbal translation and shared meaning, e.g. a wave for "hello"); illustrators (movements tied directly to accompanying speech); affect displays (facial/bodily expressions of emotion); regulators (acts that maintain and pace the back-and-forth of speaking, e.g. head nods); and adaptors (movements originally learned as adaptive habits — for managing bodily needs, emotions, or interpersonal contact — that are triggered largely outside awareness and are not communicative in intent). Adaptors are further split into self-adaptors (touching/manipulating one's own body), alter-adaptors (movements originally learned in managing contact with another person), and object-adaptors (manipulation of objects).

**Results**

As a theoretical paper, there are no quantitative results in the empirical sense. Its substantive output is the categorical scheme itself, the origin/usage/coding criteria for assigning any observed behavior to a category, and worked illustrations of how the scheme applies to naturalistic behavior documented in the authors' earlier studies.

**Conclusion**

The origin–usage–coding framework — and the adaptors category specifically — gives researchers a principled way to separate largely unintentional, low-awareness, self-directed movements (adaptors) from deliberately communicative gestures (emblems, illustrators). This distinction underlies essentially all later work treating self-touch/self-adaptors as involuntary indices of internal cognitive or emotional state rather than as intentional signals, making this paper the direct theoretical anchor for using self-adaptors as markers of discourse-planning difficulty.
